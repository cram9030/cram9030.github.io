This post is going to be the first in a multipart series. My goal is to cumulate with an assessment of Lions GM Brad Holmes similar to what I did with Martin Mayhew. I'm going to update my techniques slightly executing a lot of what I envisioned a decade ago, starting with this post updating [Chase Stewart's Aproximate Value Draft Charts](https://www.footballperspective.com/draft-value-chart/) (read more about their development in his [two](https://www.footballperspective.com/creating-a-nfl-draft-value-chart-part-i/) [part post](https://www.footballperspective.com/creating-a-draft-value-chart-part-ii/)) to the current CBA. 

The biggest difference is that when Chase Stewart developed his AV Draft Charts he was assessing on the first five years of a players performance because at the time that was the rookie contract length. The premise which I agree with being that second contracts would be at market value even if a player stays with the team so the surplus value they might be providing is primarily going to be while they are under a rookie contract. He also pointed out that the majority of players weren't on the team tha drafted them in their 6th year. The concept is still good but with the new CBA in 2020 the fifth year option on first round picks became fully guarenteed and calculated by the average salary of the 25th to 3rd highest contracts in their position and esclating from there based on play time and awards. ([Over the Cap fifth year options](https://overthecap.com/fifth-year-option-projections)) This has created a situation where only about 57% of the options were picked up according to [Over the Cap](https://overthecap.com/fifth-year-option-outcomes) where the least valuable positions and players later in the first round are most least likely to have their 5th year taken up. This means we should really only be look at the first four years of the cost controled contract where the team is likely to get value in comparison to the free agent market.

All of the work in this post is contained in the [detroit_lions_draft](https://github.com/cram9030/detroit_lions_draft) repository and the data was primarily from [pro-football-refrence](https://www.pro-football-reference.com/), [nflreadr](https://nflreadr.nflverse.com/index.html), and a little wage scale infromation from [sportrac](https://www.spotrac.com/nfl).

The first thing I did was pull every player annual Approximate Value (more information on AV can be found [here](https://www.pro-football-reference.com/about/approximate_value.htm)) who was drafted from 1970 to 2025. The next thing I wanted to do was try and recreate the original 5 year solution based off his description from [Creating a Draft Value Chart Par II](https://www.footballperspective.com/creating-a-draft-value-chart-part-ii/). You can see from the plot below that I wasn't quite able to recreate it exactly. I suspect the difference might be the treatment of the normalization of the last pick to represent marginal value of each pick over each other. In the origional post that was subtracting 3.6 AV. I did the same thing but with the second posts selection criteria of removing players seasons who produced 2AV or less prior to the fit. In my solution I normalized the last draft pick after the fit if Chase Stewart had done that prior to the filtering and fit that could explain the difference. In any case it is close enough that I felt confident moving forward with my solution.

Looking at them together you can see why Stewart chose the logrithmic one the sparsity of very low AV and the wider distribution of AV in the early few picks lends its self to that pattern but I also think a part of why it *looks* right is it *looks* like the classic Jimmy Johnson chart which is to say we have an availability bias toward the more familiar appearance. We'll that and it is actually a good fit if we compare the R^2 and RMSE.

| Fit           |   R^2  |  RMSE  |
| :-----------: | :----: | :----: |
| Exponential   | 0.2887 | 9.2179 |
| Logarithmic   | 0.2789 | 9.2809 |
| Quadratic     | 0.2805 | 9.2707 |
| Cubic         | 0.2880 | 9.2223 |
| Quartic       | 0.2892 | 9.2143 |

The first thing to note is that well none of these models are particularly good an R^2 value above 0.4 is generally considered a fairly good fit for this domain and we are pretty well below that. That being said the Logarithmic fit was the worse of all of them. The quartic was the best but probably not enough better to make up for the fact that the coefficents had higher variance so I opted to stick with the Exponential fit which is also what Massey and Thaler used in their original paper on the topic called [The Looser's Curse](https://faculty.wharton.upenn.edu/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/massey---thaler---losers-curse---management-science-july-2013.pdf).

Now that I had established the fit I wanted to codify Stewart's early attempts to compensate for what essentially is replacement level performance. I think effectively setting the last pick value to zero is not quite right because Mr. Irrelevant is still on a rookie contract and costs less that a more senior player on the vet minimum so if they on average perform even slightly better than those verterans they should be valued that way. This is where I used the [sportrac minimum salaries](https://www.spotrac.com/nfl/cba/minimum) and sorted all the players that had a single year contract within 20% of the CBA dictated minimum for their experience from 2006 to 2025 and defined those are replacement level. There were about 2807 players seasons who met that criteria with an expected annual AV of ~1.15 a table of the general statistics are below and generally support Chase Stewart's intuition.

At this point it is probably worth talking about the fact that while each of these models seem to be trying to acheive the same thing their objectives are slightly different. The Jimmy Johnson and Rich-Hill models are descriptive models while the rest are normative models. That is to say the Jimmy Johnson and Rich-Hill models are based off what GMs actually did while all the others are meant to represent what they should do ideally. 

Even within the normative models there are some nuances the first is that EAVAR and PFF WAR models are normative to the on field performance. Approximate value assigned player value for team performance in total offensive or defensive output relative to the rest of the league. The PFF WAR is meant to assign player value to the teams wins. These two values should correlate very strongly with each other and are tied directly to on field performance. The origional Football Prospectives plots fall into this category as well though there was a serious node to making choices that were more descriptive than normative when given the oppertunity like the choice of the logrithmic fit. The Fitzgerald-Spielberger model is normative in the a very clever traditional economics since where they use the second contracts as an estimate of how much the league values the player and therefore can use that to estiamte the expected value of a draft slot. I like to think of this as if GMs do not meet the Fitzgerald-Spielberger then they as a group are not being consistent with their own behavior.

With that explination out of the way I am pretty happy with the results. The plot above normalized the values so that the first picks value was 1 similar to what Timo Riske did in the PFF article. We can clearly see that for each charts with unique objectives there closer the objectives they closer they look with the Fitzgerald-Spielberger model aligning with the Football Prospective ones and the Jimmy Johnson and Rich-Hill charts lining up. 

I feel the closeness of the EAVAR and PFF-WAR models especially early in the draft is a good sign for the EAVAR model in that for all of it's flaw's PFF is still probably the best quality data for positions without counting stats like offensive lineman. We can see that the biggest difference comes in the late second round or early third round where the PFF model has a sort of two phase slope that I think if I made different modeling choices might be able to capture or it could purely be a data difference.
